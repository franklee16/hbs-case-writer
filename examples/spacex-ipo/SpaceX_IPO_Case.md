# SpaceX at $2 Trillion: Pricing the Mission

**Priya Raman, CFA**
**Senior Analyst, Growth Equity**
**Northbridge Capital Management**

**Date:** June 19, 2026

---

It is Friday afternoon, June 19, 2026, the close of the first full week of trading in SpaceX (Nasdaq: SPCX), and Priya Raman is finishing the model she will hand to Northbridge Capital's investment committee on Monday. The committee has asked her for one thing: a twelve-month price target and a position recommendation, grounded in a framework the rest of the team can stress-test. The stock closed the week near $185, down roughly $7 on the day, giving back part of a Monday spike that had pushed the company's market capitalization above $2 trillion.

Raman had read the S-1 cover to cover. What struck her was not the price action, which the financial press had covered exhaustively, but the gap between the story the world was telling about SpaceX and the story the prospectus itself told. That gap, she suspected, was where the valuation question actually lived.

She had until Monday to decide whether the committee should be overweight, market-weight, or underweight, and at what price. The easy part was describing the company. The hard part was deciding what a buyer who paid $185, or even the $135 offer price, had to believe.

## A Company Built to Go to Mars

SpaceX, founded by Elon Musk in 2002, had spent nearly its entire life private. It went public on June 12, 2026, in the largest initial public offering on record. The company priced 555.6 million shares at $135 on June 11, raising roughly $75 billion and valuing SpaceX at about $1.77 trillion at the offer. For context, that was roughly 95 times the prior year's revenue, against a net loss. The deal surpassed Saudi Aramco's 2019 listing as the largest IPO ever.

The offering had been engineered to reach individual investors. Roughly 20 to 30 percent of the deal was earmarked for retail and distributed through platforms including Robinhood, Fidelity, E*TRADE, Charles Schwab, and SoFi, several times the typical retail allocation. E*TRADE was reported as a lead retail channel. The first-day pop was large for a deal of this size but modest beside the most frenzied meme-era debuts: SPCX opened at $150, touched roughly $176 intraday, and closed at about $161, up some 19 percent. By Monday, June 15, the stock traded as high as $225.64 intraday, briefly valuing the company near $2.3 trillion, before drifting back to the $185 area by week's end. Roughly $14.4 billion was left on the table at the offer price, the largest such sum in U.S. IPO history.

The company the public thought it knew had two pillars: a launch business built on the reusable Falcon 9 and the in-development Starship, and Starlink, the satellite internet constellation. The launch business had made SpaceX the dominant orbital provider on Earth, flying more in a year than most national programs fly in a decade, carrying commercial satellites, NASA cargo and astronauts, and national-security payloads. Starship, far larger and fully reusable in design, was the vehicle on which Musk's stated long-term ambition depended. Starlink had grown into something closer to a conventional business, serving roughly 8.9 million subscribers at the end of 2025, a count that crossed 10 million in the first quarter of 2026 across more than 160 countries.

But the prospectus, Raman noticed, told a third story alongside these two.

## What the Prospectus Actually Emphasized

When Raman counted the terms, the result surprised her. In the prospectus text, the family of artificial-intelligence terms appeared far more often than the family of Mars terms, by a ratio of roughly twenty-seven to one. The word "Mars" appeared 63 times; "Martian" appeared zero times; "colony" or "colonization," five. By contrast, "artificial intelligence" appeared more than a thousand times, "compute" or "data center" more than six hundred, and "Grok" or "xAI" more than six hundred.

The addressable market the company claimed reinforced the same point. SpaceX put its total addressable market at roughly $28.5 trillion, and assigned about $26.5 trillion of it, 93 percent, to artificial intelligence. The use of proceeds named AI compute, launch vehicles, and satellites. Mars was not named among the uses of proceeds at all.

This was the angle Raman kept returning to. The marketing narrative was Mars, the romance of a multiplanetary species. The disclosure economics were largely AI. For an investor, the question was not whether Mars was inspiring; it was which business the buyer was actually funding, and how much of the $2 trillion market capitalization each business could plausibly support.

The prospectus's only precise treatment of Mars made the same point in a different register. Musk's pay package, disclosed as an exhibit to the S-1, granted him one billion Class B shares in fifteen tranches. Each tranche required either a market-capitalization milestone, rising as high as $7.5 trillion, or the "Mars Colony Milestone," defined as the establishment of a permanent human colony on Mars with at least one million inhabitants. The audited notes valued the award at $90.40 to $95.92 per share using a Monte Carlo simulation, and stated that as of March 2026 both milestones "were considered improbable," so no compensation expense was recognized. In other words, the auditors did not believe the Mars colony, nor a $7.5 trillion market cap, was likely enough to book. The award vests only in the states where the Mars bet pays, which is one reason the outside shareholder's claim on the upside tail is smaller than the per-share arithmetic suggests.

The filing also disclosed a web of related-party transactions with Tesla, Musk's electric-vehicle company, including purchases of Cybertrucks, aircraft, and battery-storage hardware (Megapacks), running into the hundreds of millions of dollars annually. The transactions were disclosed, but they raised the same governance question that ran through the whole document: Musk retained commanding voting control after the offering, so public shareholders would have little practical say in how the company was run, how capital was allocated across these businesses, or how entangled it became with its founder's other firms.

## Three Businesses, Not Two

Raman built her valuation around three segments, because the prospectus and the company's own disclosures supported three.

| Segment | 2025 Revenue | Character |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| Launch | ~$4.1B | Dominant orbital provider; government and commercial; steady but capital-light of Starship spend |
| Starlink | ~$11.4B | The cash engine; sole profitable segment; ~$4.4B segment operating income at high-tens margins |
| xAI / AI compute | ~$3.2B | The fastest-growing and most contested; sells compute and AI services |

Consolidated revenue for 2025 was about $18.7 billion, up from roughly $14.1 billion the year before, growth of approximately a third. The consolidated bottom line was a GAAP net loss of about $4.9 billion, driven substantially by non-operating and one-time charges and by heavy Starship development spend, even as the company generated roughly $6.6 billion in adjusted EBITDA and Starlink alone produced over $4 billion in segment operating income. The central fact of the financials was that one business, Starlink, was funding the other two, and one of those two, xAI, was where the prospectus said the addressable market actually was.

## The Analysis Framework

The committee did not want Raman's opinion. It wanted a framework it could flex. She built four exhibits, each of which let a reader recompute the answer under different assumptions.

The first was a segment valuation build. Calibrating revenue targets and the discount rate to the most detailed published valuation she could find, and disciplining terminal margins with listed comparables, she arrived at a base-case equity value of roughly $1.0 trillion, or about $77 per share, better read as a range of $0.86 to $1.13 trillion once the xAI scenarios were flexed. The segments stacked as follows: Starlink contributed the largest share, then xAI, then launch, plus net cash of roughly $40 billion including IPO proceeds. Every one of those inputs was sourced and could be changed.

| Segment | 2025 Rev. | 2036 Rev. Target | Term. Margin | Value |
|---------|-----------|------------------|--------------|-------|
| Launch | $4.1B | $45B | 45% | ~$139B |
| Starlink | $11.4B | $120B | 60% | ~$557B |
| xAI | $3.2B | $160B | 25% | ~$231B (range $23B–$288B) |
| Net cash incl. proceeds | | | | ~$77B |
| **Equity value** | | | | **~$1.00T (~$77/sh)** |

The second exhibit was the one she expected the committee to argue about most: a "what must the buyer believe" lever table. She held the framework at base case and solved each lever, one at a time, for the offer price. The result was uncomfortable for the bulls. To reach the $1.77 trillion offer, Starlink's terminal margin would have to be 137 percent, which has no feasible value; xAI's 2036 revenue would have to be roughly $640 billion, above the supported range and on the order of the entire AI software market; launch revenue would have to reach $266 billion, beyond every comparable. Only the discount rate stayed inside its evidence band, moving from a base 8.25 percent to an implied 6.86 percent.

| Lever | Base | Implied by $1.77T offer | Assessment |
|-------|------|--------------------------|------------|
| Starlink terminal margin | 60% | 137% | no feasible value |
| xAI 2036 revenue | $160B | ~$640B | above supported range |
| Launch 2036 revenue | $40B | ~$266B | above supported range |
| Discount rate | 8.25% | 6.86% | inside estimation error |

This was the insight Raman wanted to land on Monday. None of the cash-flow levers could close the gap to the offer price within its supported range. A discount-rate revision of 1.4 points could. The IPO debate, she had come to think, was not really a disagreement about cash flows. It was a disagreement about the price of risk, conducted in a place where estimation could not settle it.

The third exhibit made the levers concrete by translating them into physical units. To justify the price, the implied revenues required Starship to fly roughly 4.9 times per day, against a record of twelve flights total in three years; Starlink to reach roughly 359 million subscribers, against 10 million today at an average revenue per user near $66 per month; and the AI compute business to build out 128 to 640 gigawatts of capacity, against a total United States data-center load of roughly 30 to 50 gigawatts. The point was not that these were impossible. It was that the buyer at $135, let alone $185, was paying for a world in which they mostly happened.

The fourth exhibit was triangulation. Raman collected every independent value estimate she could find for June 2026 and laid them beside the offer and trading prices. Morningstar's fundamental estimate sat near $0.78 trillion; Damodaran's detailed valuation near $1.25 to $1.35 trillion; secondary-market trading platforms near $1.3 to $1.8 trillion; prediction markets and pre-listing futures near $2.1 trillion; the offer at $1.77 trillion; and the first trading day, which closed near $2.11 trillion after touching $2.31 trillion intraday. The offer sat above the fundamental estimates and at the top of the private-trading range. The market, in the first week, had traded above all of them.

| Source | Equity value estimate |
|--------|----------------------|
| Morningstar (fundamental) | ~$0.78T |
| Damodaran (fundamental, detailed) | ~$1.25–1.35T |
| Secondary private platforms | ~$1.3–1.8T |
| **Offer price** | **$1.77T** |
| Prediction markets / futures (June 11) | ~$2.1T |
| First trading day close (June 12) | ~$2.11T (touched ~$2.31T) |

Raman also flagged two structural facts the committee needed. First, duration: roughly 87 percent of operating value sat in the terminal claim, giving the equity an effective duration of about twenty-nine years, so value responded roughly linearly to cash-flow revisions but steeply to rate revisions, about a third of value per point of required return. Second, the precision asymmetry: cash-flow inputs were pinned tightly by comparables and addressable markets, while the discount rate carried a standard error north of three points a year, so the 1.4-point rate revision that closed the gap to the offer was within one standard error and the evidence could not reject it. The disagreement was real, and the data could not adjudicate it.

## Risk Factors

The S-1 risk factors, grouped, fell into four clusters the committee would recognize. The first was key-person and governance risk: dependence on Musk, his split attention across multiple public companies, and his commanding voting control, which left public shareholders with limited practical say. The second was concentration: Starlink was the sole profitable segment and was effectively funding launch and xAI, so any slowdown in subscriber growth, continued decline in average revenue per user as the service pushed into price-sensitive markets, or competitive pressure from rival satellite and terrestrial providers would compound quickly. The third was capital intensity and optionality: Starship represented enormous expenditure with no guarantee of commercial success and a repeatedly slipping timeline, and a Mars program had no near-term revenue. The fourth was entanglement: the related-party transactions with Tesla and the milestone-gated pay award tied the company's economics to its founder's other firms and ambitions.

## The Decision

Raman looked at the four exhibits and then at the price on her screen. The framework did not hand her a single answer, but it narrowed the question sharply. Her base case, anchored to sourced segment targets and disciplined margins, pointed to a value of roughly $1.0 trillion, about $77 per share, with a defensible range of $0.86 to $1.13 trillion. Against a market price near $185 and an offer of $135, that was a wide gap. The only lever that closed it without leaving its evidence band was the discount rate, which meant the buyer at the market was making an implicit bet on the price of risk, not on cash flows the evidence supported.

That gave her three live recommendations for the committee, each defensible. She could recommend underweight with a target near her base case, on the view that the cash-flow evidence did not support the price and that the first-week strength reflected flows and sentiment that would mean-revert. She could recommend market-weight, arguing that the discount-rate bet was within the evidence the data could not reject, that Nasdaq-100 inclusion roughly fifteen trading days after listing would add mechanical demand at around three times the float weight, and that the dispersion of estimates was wide enough to humility her base case. Or she could recommend overweight on the xAI optionality, treating the $23 billion to $288 billion span on the AI business as a real option worth paying for, on the view that the listed-comparable margins understated what a winning frontier model could earn.

Each recommendation turned on a different belief: that the price of risk would normalize, that flows would persist, or that the AI option was in the money. The framework let the committee disagree precisely, rather than vaguely. What it did not do was tell her which belief was right.

She saved the model and closed her laptop. On Monday she would have to pick one, and defend it.

Was SpaceX a $1 trillion company the market had bid to $2 trillion on sentiment and flows, or a $2 trillion company whose base-case segment build simply could not see the optionality the buyer was paying for? And how much of the premium, Raman wondered, was being absorbed by buyers who did not realize they were making a bet on the price of risk at all?
